## What makes EVOO different

Most people treat olive oil as a cooking fat or condiment. High-polyphenol EVOO is better understood as a functional food — closer to a medicine than a standard cooking oil. The key compound responsible for its therapeutic effects is [[oleocanthal]], which acts on the same anti-inflammatory enzymes as ibuprofen.

## Quality markers

The majority of supermarket EVOO in the US may not meet true extra-virgin standards. A 2010 UC Davis study found most tested imported EVOO failed international authenticity standards — some diluted with cheaper oils, some rancid or oxidized.

| Quality indicator | What to look for |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Throat burn | Peppery burn and mild cough when sipped straight — caused by [[oleocanthal]]. Smooth = low polyphenols. |
| Bottle type | Dark glass only. Clear or plastic bottles accelerate oxidation. |
| Harvest date | Fresher is better. Polyphenols degrade quickly after pressing. |
| Process | Cold-pressed, single-origin, early harvest = highest polyphenol density |

## Key compounds

- **[[Oleocanthal]]**: Anti-inflammatory polyphenol; inhibits COX-1 and COX-2 like ibuprofen
- **Oleic acid**: Primary monounsaturated fatty acid; supports heart health and insulin signaling
- **Oleuropein / hydroxytyrosol**: Additional antioxidant and anti-inflammatory polyphenols

## Metabolic effects

- **Insulin sensitivity**: Improves insulin signaling; reduces post-meal blood sugar spikes (one study: 20%+ reduction with 10g EVOO at a meal)
- **Visceral fat**: [[predimed-study]] found Mediterranean diet + EVOO reduced waist circumference more than a low-fat diet
- **Satiety**: Triggers GLP-1 signaling pathway hormones (CCK, peptide YY, leptin) — natural appetite suppression
- **Thermogenesis**: May increase diet-induced calorie burn by ~5.1% vs. other diets
- **Inflammation**: Reduces [[metabolic-inflammation]] markers (IL-6, TNF-alpha); associated with visceral fat reduction

## Recommended daily dose

Studies and the [[olive-oil-daily-protocol]] suggest 2–4 tbsp/day for meaningful metabolic effects. The [[predimed-study]] used 4 tbsp/day. Azadi's experiment used 3 tbsp/day.

## Cooking vs. raw consumption

High heat degrades polyphenols. For therapeutic anti-inflammatory benefit, consuming EVOO raw — between meals, taken as shots — is more effective than using it as a cooking oil.
