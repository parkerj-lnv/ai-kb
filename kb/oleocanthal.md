# Oleocanthal

#health

## What it is

Oleocanthal is a naturally occurring phenolic compound in freshly pressed [[extra-virgin-olive-oil]], identified in a 2005 study published in *Nature* by researchers at the Monell Chemical Senses Center.

## How it was discovered

A researcher formulating a liquid ibuprofen noticed the same throat-burning sensation when tasting fresh olive oil at a meal in Sicily. Investigation confirmed both substances act on the same receptor pathways (COX-1 and COX-2).

## Mechanism

Oleocanthal inhibits **COX-1 and COX-2** cyclooxygenase enzymes — the same targets as NSAIDs like ibuprofen.

- ~50g of high-quality EVOO (~3.5 tbsp) provides anti-inflammatory activity equivalent to roughly **10% of an adult ibuprofen dose** per day
- Unlike ibuprofen: no liver damage, no gut lining destruction, no standard NSAID side effects

## The throat test

The peppery burn and mild cough when drinking fresh EVOO straight is caused by oleocanthal — the single best field indicator of polyphenol content:

- **Burns / causes coughing** → high oleocanthal → quality oil
- **Goes down smooth** → low oleocanthal → polyphenols likely degraded or oil adulterated

## Health associations

- **Type 2 diabetes**: Mediterranean diet high in oleocanthal-rich EVOO associated with up to 51% reduction in T2D risk in cited studies (source: [[yt-olive-oil-10-day-experiment]])
- **Visceral fat / inflammation**: 2020 study (23 adults, metabolic syndrome) — 2 months of high-oleocanthal EVOO reduced IL-6, TNF-alpha, waist circumference, visceral fat ratio, and fatty liver index
- **Joint discomfort**: Anti-inflammatory effect may reduce chronic joint pain without NSAID side effects

## Degradation factors

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Time since harvest | Polyphenols drop quickly; fresh-pressed is better |
| Light exposure | Clear bottles accelerate degradation |
| Heat | High-heat cooking destroys polyphenol content |
| Oxidation | Rancid or poorly stored oil has near-zero oleocanthal |
